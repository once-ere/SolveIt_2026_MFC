Motion, Displacement and Velocity

We are going to learn how physicists describe the motion of an object, such as a thrown ball. Imagine standing on top of a flat level cliff near the edge and throwing a ball toward the edge so that it hits the ground several feet before the edge. Mark the spot where you are standing. We'll call this spot the origin. Also mark the spot where the ball hits. Draw a straight line through these two spots. This line will be called our x-axis. We'll assume that you have drawn a very long line that passes through the two points, and keeps going forever in both directions!

Before we really start describing the motion of the ball let's come up with a few more reference points.

Suppose you mentally draw a line straight up through the point where you are standing (the origin). This line will be called our y-axis. We'll assume that you have a lot of energy and have drawn the line so that it passes down through the cliff all the way to the center of the Earth, and back out the other side of the Earth into outer space.

The world has three spatial dimensions. This means that through any chosen point, it is possible to draw three lines that are perpendicular to one another. For example, place a child's toy block on your desk, and pick a point on your desk where one of the corners of the block is. The three edges of the block that intersect at this corner point determine the three lines. You could define the spatial dimension of the world as the number of edges leading away from a cubical block vertex (corner). Since the world has three spatial dimensions we need three axes to locate points in our environment. The third axis is called the z-axis. We won't need this axis yet, but if you look carefully you can find it in Figure 1.

Back to the ball. The motion of the ball is first upward then downward toward the cliff edge. Toward the cliff edge means along the first line you drew. It means along the x-axis.

The upward and downward motion has taken place along the y-axis. Now, the two lines that determine our x and y-axes lie in a flat two-dimensional space the we call a plane. We call this plane your x-y plane (what else? the y-x plane??).

Next, wind up and throw the ball over the cliff, but in the same direction as before (along the x-axis). The motion of the ball takes place in the x-y plane. This motion is the ball's trajectory. The trajectory is illustrated in Figure 1. Click the check box labeled "trajectory" to see a graph of this motion in the x-y plane. With the trajectory check box selected, press the button labeled "animate" to see a slow-motion depiction (simulation) of the ball's motion over the cliff.

Figure 1: Trajectory of ball thrown over cliff


Now let's bring time into the picture. Suppose you throw the ball over the cliff at time zero. Let's write "time zero" as t = 0 s, where the "s" is the abbreviation for "seconds". You throw the ball at t = 0 s and it goes over the cliff.

The ball moves upward for a while, then falls back down. Let's assume that it takes three tenths (0.3) of a second for the ball to reach its highest point. The ball reachs its highest point at t = 0.3 s. Then the ball begins to fall back down, toward the center of the earth. Of course, it speeds up as it falls because of gravity. We will discuss this speeding up and the direction of the fall in a later chapter. The point we will focus on here is that at different times the ball is at different places. At different times the ball is at different points of its trajectory. If you click the check box labeled "displacement vector" [with the trajectory check box checked (selected)] you can see a magenta arrow that starts from the point from which you threw the ball, and locates the ball at a later time. This arrow starts from the origin and ends at the position of the ball at a later time.

This magenta arrow is called the displacement vector. The length of the arrow is the distance from you (really your feet) to the ball. It is the distance from the origin to the ball. This length is also called the magnitude of the vector. This magnitude or distance is often measured in feet or meters. We will use meters. The abbreviation for "meters" is "m". For example, if the distance from your feet to the ball is six meters, the we say that the magnitude of the displacement vector = 6 m.

The arrow points toward the ball. If it pointed in a different direction, then it would not end at the ball! The direction of the displacement vector is just as important as its magnitude. Change its magnitude (length) and the arrow is either too short or too long, and does not touch the ball. Change its direction, and the arrow points in the wrong direction, and does not touch the ball.

It is important to realize that the ball moves along its trajectory, not along the displacement vector! If you wanted to shoot the ball with a laser beam, then the laser beam would move along the displacement vector. In this example, the ball does not follow the displacement vector!

Let's describe how fast and in what direction the ball is moving. We're talking velocity, the velocity vector. What we ultimately seek is a velocity vector with two special properties:
  • The direction of the velocity vector is the direction that the ball is moving.
  • The magnitude of the velocity vector is just the speed of the ball.

Unfortunately, we cannot just jump right in and define the velocity vector. We first must discuss a "helper" quantity (velocity helper) called the average velocity vector. The average velocity vector is defined to be the displacement vector locating the ball at time t, divided by t. Suppose t = 0.5s . When you divide the arrow representing the displacement vector by 0.5s you get another arrow that points in the same direction, but which has a different length. When you divide a vector (an arrow) by a positive number like time, you get another vector (arrow) pointing in the same direction, but with a different magnitude (length).

Because of its definition, the average velocity vector has to be parallel to the displacement vector. This is because the two arrows point in exactly the same direction; they just have different lengths. The average velocity vector simply has a different magnitude and different units than the displacement vector . The magnitude of the average velocity vector is measured in meters per second, m/s, and may or may not have any meaningful physical interpretation; this depends on the details of the motion. The magnitude of the displacement vector is measured in meters , m, and is the distance of the ball from the origin.

It is important to notice that the displacement vector can be pointing in one direction and the ball can be moving in a completely different direction. Only just after you have thrown the ball are the two pointing in the roughly same direction. The same is true for the average velocity vector. It too can point in a direction different from the direction that the ball is moving. Only just after t = 0s is the average velocity vector pointing in the roughly same direction as the ball is moving.

Your common sense is probably complaining right now. Intuitively you would like the velocity vector of an object to be pointing in the direction in which the object is moving. The key observation is that near t = 0s the displacement vector is roughly pointed in the direction that the ball is moving. Maybe we could move to a position along the trajectory of the ball corresponding to, say, t = 0.5s and watch as the ball passes through this point. The displacement vector defined relative to this position points in the direction that the ball is moving while t is just a little more than 0.5s .

This idea prompts us to define the relative displacement vector that locates the ball at say, t=0.6s, relative to the position of the ball at t=0.5s . If you click the check box labeled "relative displacement vector" (with the displacement vector check box checked) then you will see a cyan arrow that represents the relative displacement vector. It connects the displacement vector at an earlier time to the displacement vector at a later time (the two magenta arrows). Try this with the trajectory check box checked (selected) and then unchecked (not selected).

In order to extend this idea a little further, let us call t = 0.5 s the initial time ti, and call the time t = 0.6s the final time tf . The time difference is (the final time ) - (the initial time ) = 0.6s - 0.5s = 0.1s = tf - ti. The relative displacement vector is constructed by locating the ball at time 0.5s and drawing an arrow to where the ball is at time 0.6s. The relative displacement vector is found by subtracting the displacement vector at the initial time0.5s from the displacement vector at the final time 0.6s. The velocity vector is equal to this relative displacement vector divided by the time difference 0.1s.

The relative displacement vector is constructed by locating the ball at time ti and drawing an arrow to where the ball is at time tf. The relative displacement vector is found by subtracting the displacement vector at the initial time ti from the displacement vector at the final time tf. The velocity vector is equal to this relative displacement vector divided by the time difference tf - ti.

The velocity is the relative displacement of the ball per unit time.

Actually this is only a good approximation to the velocity vector. The exact velocity is obtained by allowing the final time tf to slip back toward the initial time ti until it just barely exceeds it. Try to visualize this. The two displacement vectors get very close together, of course. Use your mental powers to zoom in on these two vectors and get a magnified view of their appearance. In the future we'll assume that you mentally slid tf back toward ti without being prompted.

The direction of the velocity vector is the direction of the dark cyan arrow. The direction of the velocity vector is the direction that the ball is moving. Since velocity is a vector, it has a magnitude as well as a direction. The magnitude of the velocity vector is just the speed of the ball. The velocity vector changes whenever the direction in which the ball is moving changes, and/or whenever the speed of the ball changes. If you tie the ball to one end of a string and grab the other end of the string and swing the ball in a circle above your head at a constant rate (speed), then the velocity is constantly changing since the direction that the ball is moving in is continually changing.
